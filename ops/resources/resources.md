# The Complete Consulting Toolkit

A comprehensive guide to consulting mindset, stakeholder management, interviewing, competency preparation, and client-site success.

---

## Part 1: The Consulting Mindset

### What Does a Consultant Actually Do?

A consultant is **not** the decision-maker. A consultant is the person who helps others make better decisions.

**Core responsibilities:**
- Understand the current situation (what's really happening)
- Define the desired future state (what should be happening)
- Identify the gap between the two
- Recommend and support the changes needed to close it

### The Consultant's Superpower: Objectivity

You see what insiders can't — because you're not embedded in the politics, habits, or history.

**Your value comes from:**
- Asking the right questions (not having all the answers)
- Structured thinking (frameworks over gut instinct)
- Translating between business and technology
- Professional scepticism (challenge assumptions politely)

### The Business Analyst Role

A Business Analyst is the bridge between the business problem and the solution.

**Core responsibilities:**
- Investigating and modelling business situations
- Identifying stakeholders and understanding their needs
- Defining and managing requirements
- Analysing options and recommending solutions
- Supporting change and benefits realisation

As an AI consultant, you do all of this — with the added challenge of explaining what's technically possible to people who may not understand AI.

### The Foundational Mental Model: As-Is → Gap → To-Be

Almost every consulting engagement follows this structure:

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **As-Is** | Where are we now? Current situation, processes, pain points |
| **To-Be** | Where do we want to be? Desired future state |
| **The Gap** | What needs to change to get from here to there? This gap IS the project |

#### MoneyCraft Example

| As-Is (Today) | To-Be (Project Insight 2025) |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| 80 data specialists scattered across 8 departments | Centralised Data and Analytics Hub in Canary Wharf |
| Each department uses different data tools | Standardised tools and practices |
| Inconsistent data practices and limited knowledge sharing | Cross-functional collaboration and clear career paths |
| Difficulty retaining data talent | Competitive edge in the fintech landscape |

### Delivery Approaches

#### Waterfall (Sequential)
- Plan everything upfront → Build → Test → Deliver
- Requirements defined early, change managed formally

#### Agile (Iterative)
- Deliver in short cycles (sprints)
- Requirements evolve, feedback continuous, adapt as you learn

> Most AI projects lean agile — because you often don't know what's possible until you start experimenting with the data.

#### How Your Role Shifts

| Area | Waterfall | Agile |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Requirements | Big document upfront | User stories, evolving backlog |
| Stakeholder Engagement | Formal sign-off gates | Continuous collaboration |
| Requirements Management | Formal change requests | Expected each sprint |

*In practice, most projects use a hybrid. Your job is to adapt.*

### Your Consulting Toolkit

Core skills every consultant needs:
- **Stakeholder Management** — who matters, what they need, how to engage them
- **Strategic Analysis** — SWOT, PESTLE, understanding the landscape
- **Requirements Engineering** — capturing what needs to be built
- **Change Management** — why people resist and how to support them
- **The Business Case** — justifying the investment
- **Communication** — interviewing, presenting, facilitating

## Part 2: Stakeholder Management

### What is a Stakeholder?

**BCS Definition:** A person, group, or organisation that has an interest in, or may be affected by, a situation and its outcome.

> **Key insight:** Stakeholders aren't just the people who asked for the project. They include anyone who will be affected by the change — even if they don't know about it yet.

In AI projects, this is especially important. The people whose work will be changed by AI are stakeholders, whether or not anyone has told them.

### Identifying Stakeholders

Ask yourself: who…

- Requested or sponsors the project?
- Will use the solution?
- Will be affected by the change?
- Has the power to block or enable it?
- Provides subject matter expertise?
- Needs to approve, regulate, or govern it?
- Will deliver or build the solution?

> Cast the net wide at first. You can always narrow down later — but a missed stakeholder is a risk.

### MoneyCraft Stakeholders

Project Insight 2025 — centralising the data team:

| Stakeholder | Role |
|-------------|------|
| CEO Cristina Yang | Sponsor and advocate |
| 80 data specialists | Being relocated |
| Department heads | Losing "their" data people |
| Lending and Credit / Digital Banking | Already concerned |
| Banking Technology team | Not directly impacted |
| HR | Managing the transition |
| Risk Management and Compliance | Regulatory implications |
| Customers | Indirectly affected |
| The FCA | Regulatory oversight |

### Mendelow's Power/Interest Grid

The most widely used stakeholder analysis tool. Maps stakeholders on two axes:

- **Power:** How much influence do they have over the project?
- **Interest:** How much do they care about the outcome?

```
                    High Power
                        │
        Keep Satisfied │ Manage Closely
        (e.g., The FCA)│ (e.g., CEO Cristina Yang)
                        │
────────────────────────┼────────────────────────
                        │
        Monitor         │ Keep Informed
        (e.g., Customers)│ (e.g., Data specialists)
                        │
                    Low Power
```

| Quadrant | Strategy |
|----------|----------|
| **High Power, High Interest** | Manage Closely — regular engagement, involve in decisions |
| **High Power, Low Interest** | Keep Satisfied — keep informed, don't overwhelm |
| **Low Power, High Interest** | Keep Informed — regular updates, listen to concerns |
| **Low Power, Low Interest** | Monitor — minimal effort, light-touch communication |

> The grid is a living tool — stakeholders can move between quadrants as the project evolves.

### Tailoring Your Engagement

| Factor | Approach |
|--------|----------|
| **Communication frequency** | Daily stand-ups for key players vs monthly updates for monitors |
| **Communication channel** | Face-to-face for sensitive conversations, email for routine updates |
| **Level of detail** | Executive summary for senior stakeholders, detailed specs for delivery teams |
| **Involvement in decisions** | Co-creation with key players, consultation with others, notification for the rest |

> **The golden rule:** no stakeholder should ever be surprised.

### When Stakeholders Conflict

Stakeholders rarely all want the same thing.

**At MoneyCraft:**
- CEO wants centralisation for competitive advantage
- Department heads want to keep "their" data specialists
- Data specialists want career growth but may not want to relocate
- Compliance needs regulatory standards maintained during transition

**Your role as consultant:**
- Make conflicts visible (don't pretend they don't exist)
- Facilitate resolution — don't pick sides
- Escalate when you can't resolve
- Document decisions and rationale

### The RASCI Matrix

A tool for clarifying who does what. Prevents the "I thought you were doing that" problem.

| Letter | Role | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **R** | Responsible | Does the work |
| **A** | Accountable | Owns the outcome. **Only ONE person per task** |
| **S** | Supportive | Provides support and assistance |
| **C** | Consulted | Provides input before work (two-way communication) |
| **I** | Informed | Told about outcome after the fact (one-way communication) |

> **The critical rule:** every task must have exactly one A.

#### Common RASCI Mistakes

- **Multiple A's** — if everyone is accountable, nobody is
- **No A at all** — the task falls through the cracks
- **Too many C's** — decision-making grinds to a halt
- **Confusing R and A** — doer vs. owner
- **Forgetting to include the right I's** — blindsided stakeholders become blockers

> A well-built RASCI is a communication tool, not just an admin exercise. It surfaces gaps, overlaps, and misunderstandings before they become problems.

## Part 3: Interviewing & Active Listening

> The skill that separates good consultants from great ones.

### Why Interviewing Matters

Interviews are the primary way consultants gather information.

**What you get from a good interview:**
- Requirements you won't find in any document
- The real reasons behind decisions and processes
- Political dynamics and hidden concerns
- Trust and rapport with stakeholders
- Early warning signals about resistance

**What you get from a bad interview:**
- Surface-level answers that confirm what you already knew
- A stakeholder who feels their time was wasted
- Missed requirements that come back to haunt you later

### Question Types

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Open questions** | Invite exploration. Start with what, how, why, tell me about | "Tell me about how you currently process data requests." |
| **Closed questions** | Confirm specific facts. Get yes/no or short answer | "Do all departments use the same reporting tool?" |
| **Probing questions** | Dig deeper into something they've said | "You mentioned the handoff is slow — can you walk me through what happens?" |
| **Leading questions** | Suggest the answer (AVOID) | "Don't you think the current system is too slow?" |

> **Best practice:** start open, then probe, then close to confirm.

### Structuring an Interview

#### Before
- Research the interviewee — role, department, likely concerns
- Prepare 5–8 open questions (you won't use them all)
- Share the agenda in advance

#### During
- Introduce yourself and the purpose clearly
- Start broad ("tell me about your role") before getting specific
- Listen more than you talk — aim for 70/30
- Take notes but maintain eye contact
- Summarise back what you've heard before moving on

#### After
- Write up notes within 24 hours (memory decays fast)
- Send a summary to the interviewee for validation
- Identify gaps and plan follow-up questions

### Active Listening Techniques

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Paraphrasing** | "So what you're saying is…" — proves you understood |
| **Reflecting** | "It sounds like that's been frustrating" — acknowledges emotion |
| **Clarifying** | "When you say 'the old system', do you mean…?" — removes ambiguity |
| **Summarising** | "Let me check I've got this right…" — confirms understanding |
| **Comfortable silence** | Don't rush to fill pauses. People often share the most useful information after a moment of silence. |

### Common Interviewing Mistakes

- Asking leading questions — you get the answer you planted, not the truth
- Talking too much — the interviewee should do 70% of the talking
- Jumping to solutions — "have you tried…?" shuts down exploration
- Not following up — the best insights come from probing
- Writing everything down verbatim — you miss body language and tone
- Interviewing in pairs without agreeing roles — one leads, one notes
- Not validating your notes — your interpretation may not match their intent

> **The biggest mistake:** assuming you already know the answer and using the interview to confirm it.

### Key Takeaways

1. Use open questions to explore, probing to dig deeper, closed to confirm
2. Structure interviews: prepare, listen (70/30 rule), write up and validate
3. Active listening is deliberate — paraphrase, reflect, clarify, summarise
4. Comfortable silence is your friend
5. The most important information is often what people don't say unprompted
6. Always validate your notes with the interviewee afterwards

## Part 4: Competency Preparation

### Skills vs Competencies

| Skills | Competencies |
|--------|--------------|
| Specific, tangible abilities you've learnt | Broader combination of skills, knowledge, behaviours, attributes |
| Task specific | Situation and role specific |
| Technical or non-technical | Higher level of complexity |
| Fundamental building blocks | Encompass multiple skills |

**Examples:**
- **Skill:** Programming in Python, speaking French, writing reports
- **Competency:** Communications, adaptability, teamwork, leadership

Competencies are a set of skills bound together by:
- Experience
- Behaviours
- Reflections

> Skills focus on tasks; competencies focus on the overarching role.

### What is a Competency Interview?

A behavioural focused interview that assesses a candidate's:
- Skills
- Abilities
- Behaviours

The interview measures the alignment of past experience and motivations required to be successful in the role.

**Common competencies assessed:**
- Teamwork
- Problem-solving
- Communication
- Adaptability
- Leadership

### The STARR Framework

| Component | Description | Detail |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **S** - Situation | Give context to set the scene | 1-2 points |
| **T** - Task | Describe the purpose/problem to solve | 1 point |
| **A** - Action | Describe steps taken and highlight associated skills | 3-5 points (at least 1 per skill) |
| **R** - Result | State a measurable outcome | 1 point |
| **R** - Reflection | Demonstrate growth mindset from the experience | 1 point |

### The "Tell Me About Yourself" Structure

Keep it brief. There's ample time to discuss experiences throughout the interview.

| Component | Time | Content |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Who am I now** | ~10 seconds | Short story about what you've accomplished in the academy |
| **How did I get here** | ~10 seconds | What did you do in the past? How did you decide to enter technology? |
| **What do I aspire to do** | 1 sentence | Personal to you (think about your how & why) |
| **What are my interests** | 1 sentence | Separates you from other candidates |

> Start with a simple polite — "Thank you for this opportunity" — always sets you up for success.

### Tell Me About Your Time at Digital Futures

**Structure:**
- Digital Futures is a 12-week program specialising in [Pathway] and professional skills
- We focused on [major theme chronologically]
- We learnt [competencies/technologies X, Y, Z]
- We applied these to real-world challenges such as…
- From this journey I've learnt [insert reflection here]

**Must include:**
- Professional skills
- Real-world application
- Your journey and growth

### Key Signals to Convey in Interviews

Clients are looking for potential, adaptability, and to know they can work with you.

| Signal | Why It Matters | How to Show It |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Passion for Technology** | Shows you didn't fall into this field by accident; you're here to build a career | Talk about personal projects, what excites you about tech |
| **Curiosity** | Key to understanding environment, empathy for co-workers and customers | Ask questions, mention learning materials (YouTube, podcasts, hands-on projects) |
| **Growth Mindset** | You won't know everything, but you'll absorb knowledge | Self-reflection: what did I learn? Embrace challenges, view failure as learning |
| **Proactivity** | Ability to perform tasks autonomously | Examples of taking initiative, thinking one step ahead |

### Company Alignment

Research the following in depth:
- **Mission**
- **Goals**
- **Culture**

> Tip: Referencing facts and figures based on news articles can be very effective.

### Asking Questions at Interview

Your question is more important than their response.

**Structure:**
1. **The set up** — 1 sentence statement
2. **The question** — open-ended

| Non-effective | Effective |
|---------------|-----------|
| "What does progress look like for me?" | "From my research, it's clear [company] prides themselves on developing talent. Can you give me more information on what progress looks like?" |

**How many questions to prepare:** 4-5 total
- 1-2 about the company
- 1-2 about the team
- 1-2 role specific

> Tip 1: Have your notebook out — it shows you've prepared.
> Tip 2: Be seen taking notes to show eagerness to learn.

### Why Competency is Part of Graduation

- **95%** of interviews contain competency assessment
- Data from over 695 client interviews via Digital Futures
- Success with client interviews is strongly linked to high performance in competency interviews

**This is not a mock interview — it's a seal of approval** that ensures each trainee meets the high standards clients expect.

**Who will I have my competency with?**

## Part 5: What to Expect on Client Site

### Why Client Site is Different

| In Training | On Client Site |
|-------------|----------------|
| Clear exercises with defined outcomes | Ambiguous requests with shifting goals |
| One instructor, structured curriculum | Multiple stakeholders, competing priorities |
| Safe space to ask questions | Pressure to "know" and deliver quickly |
| Predictable schedule | Urgent, unpredictable demands |
| Mistakes are learning opportunities | Mistakes affect project delivery & trust |

### What Success Looks Like

**Sarah — 6 months on client site:**
- Gets vague requests but asks 3-4 clarifying questions before starting
- When overloaded, negotiates priorities openly instead of silently struggling
- Escalated twice in first 3 months — both times with clear evidence and impact
- Built trust by being transparent about what she knows and doesn't know

**James — 1 year on client site:**
- Known for "thoughtful questions that save the team time"
- Uses "yes, with conditions" language to manage stakeholder expectations
- Documents assumptions in writing so no one is surprised when things change
- Stays calm under pressure by focusing on what he can control

> **The pattern:** They ask clarifying questions, negotiate workload instead of over-promising, escalate early with facts, and stay professional when emotions run high.

### Challenge 1: Navigating Ambiguity

**What ambiguity looks like:**
- Unclear instructions
- Stakeholders changing their minds
- Missing/conflicting information
- Vague project goals
- Unclear definition of "done"
- Unrealistic deadlines

**How to deal with ambiguity:**

1. **Clarify** — Ask the right questions:
   - What is the purpose?
   - Where does this fit in the bigger picture?
   - When is the deadline?
   - Why are we doing this?
   - Who are the stakeholders?
   - How do we move forward given what we know?

2. **Control** — Focus on what you can influence:
   - Schedule time for thinking and planning
   - Document assumptions and decisions
   - Focus on actionable items

3. **Act** — Move forward with what you know:
   - Make progress even with partial information
   - Be transparent about assumptions made
   - Iterate and adapt as new information emerges

> **Note:** The cons of not asking usually outweigh any hesitation about speaking up.

### Challenge 2: Managing Workload and Prioritisation

Often, the struggle isn't the work itself; it's the volume and lack of clear priorities.

**Why this is hard:**
- Multiple "urgent" asks
- Vague deadlines like "ASAP" and "by end of week"
- Pressure to say yes to everything

#### Three Steps to Handle Multiple Demands

1. **LIST** — Write down everything on your plate
2. **SORT** — Use Eisenhower Matrix (Urgent vs. Important)
3. **NEGOTIATE** — Communicate openly using "yes, with conditions"

#### The "Yes, With Conditions" Framework

| Approach | Context | Language |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **Peer/Colleague** | Same level | "I can review that deck. I have two other tasks due tomorrow, can we align on which is more urgent?" |
| **Your Manager** | Direct supervisor | "I can take this on. Here's what's currently on my plate: Task A (due Friday), Task B (draft stage). Which should I prioritise?" |
| **Senior Stakeholder** | Client leadership | "I can pull those numbers. To give you something robust, I'd need until 2pm tomorrow. Does that work, or would a high-level summary by 5pm today be more helpful?" |

### Challenge 3: Knowing When and How to Escalate

**Escalation** is passing an issue to a higher level of authority, expertise, or management when it cannot be resolved at the current level.

> It is not "snitching" or "giving up"; it is a structured way to protect delivery, quality, and relationships when your options are exhausted.

#### The Escalation Spectrum

| Too Early (Don't Escalate Yet) | Sweet Spot (Escalate Now) | Too Late (Should Have Escalated) |
|-------------------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Haven't tried any solutions | Tried 2+ reasonable approaches | Deadline has passed |
| Issue just emerged | Clear risk to delivery/quality | Escalating in panic, not with facts |
| Haven't spoken to person directly | Decision needed beyond your role | Damage has already occurred |
| No clear impact yet | Have evidence and can articulate stakes | Manager says "Why didn't you tell me?" |

#### The ESC Model

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **E** - Evidence | What factually happened |
| **S** - Stakes | Why it matters (risk, deadline, quality impact) |
| **C** - Call to action | What you need (decision, support, clarification) |

> **Do:** Escalate with evidence, not emotion.
> **Don't:** Wait until it's too late or hope the issue resolves itself.

### Challenge 4: Professionalism in Emotional Situations

Client environments can be fast-paced, pressured, and sometimes chaotic. People may show stress, urgency, or frustration more openly than you're used to.

#### The CALMER Framework

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **C** - Consider | What triggered this? What's the context? |
| **A** - Acknowledge | Acknowledge emotions (yours and theirs) without necessarily agreeing |
| **L** - Listen | Listen actively to understand their perspective fully |
| **M** - Manage | Manage your emotions; decide what can be done now, set boundaries |
| **E** - Enable | Outline options and support decision-making |
| **R** - Resource | Signpost further support and look after your own wellbeing |

### What Makes a Good Consultant On-Site

- Ask clarifying questions when direction is unclear — even when it feels uncomfortable
- Negotiate openly when workload is unrealistic — instead of silently drowning
- Escalate with evidence when options are exhausted — not in panic when it's too late
- Stay calm under pressure — by managing emotions, not matching them
- Own mistakes and recover professionally — clients respect resilience, not perfection

> **Use your Power of Being New:** Your newness is an asset, not a weakness. You see things others have stopped noticing. You ask questions that challenge assumptions. You bring a fresh perspective.
>
> Clients don't expect you to know everything on day one. They expect you to be curious, honest, and willing to learn.

## Part 6: Giving and Receiving Feedback

### Why Feedback Matters

Feedback:
- Prevents performance drift
- Protects reputation
- Builds self-awareness
- Creates accountability
- Builds trust
- Accelerates growth

### Definition of Professional Feedback

Professional feedback is information about observable behaviour that helps improve performance or reinforce impact.

It:
- Focuses on observable behaviour
- Aims to improve performance or reinforce impact
- Is tied to business outcomes
- Focuses on behaviour, not personality

### Where Feedback Happens on Client Site

- Sprint reviews
- Code reviews
- Retrospectives
- 1:1s
- Teams comments
- Performance reviews

### What Client Feedback Looks Like

**Real-world examples:**
- "Can we tighten this up?"
- "This needs more rigour."
- "Let's revisit the assumptions."
- "We'll need this faster next time."
- "You may want to rethink that approach."

> Feedback may be brief, delivered in public, written, indirect (especially in UK corporate culture), or feel blunt in technical teams.

### Why Feedback Can Feel Exposing

Feedback can challenge:
- Our competence
- Our status
- Our identity
- Our belonging

The brain doesn't easily distinguish between physical threat and social threat. Feedback can trigger:
- Defensiveness
- Withdrawal
- Over-explaining
- Justifying
- Emotional shutdown

> This is normal. It is biological.

### The Amygdala Response

| Response | Behaviour |
|----------|-----------|
| **Fight** | Argue, justify, interrupt, deflect |
| **Flight** | Shut down, withdraw, disengage |
| **Freeze** | Go blank, nod without processing |

> Professional maturity = noticing this reaction and choosing a response.

### Ego vs Identity

Feedback is hard when we attach it to identity:

| Identity-Based | Behaviour-Based |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "I am incompetent" | "I made a mistake" |

> Consultants must learn to separate performance from identity and see feedback as data, not judgement.

### Types of Feedback

| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Appreciation** | Encouragement |
| **Coaching** | Development |
| **Evaluation** | Performance |

### Professional Response Frameworks

#### Framework 1: ARCA

Use when feedback requires follow-up, you need clarity, or the conversation is ongoing.

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **A** - Acknowledge | "Thank you — that's helpful." |
| **R** - Reflect | Consider the feedback |
| **C** - Clarify | "Could you give me an example so I can understand how to improve?" |
| **A** - Act | Commit to action |

#### Framework 2: The 3R Model

Use when feedback catches you off guard.

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Receive** | Without interruption |
| **Respond** | With ownership |
| **Reframe** | Into behaviour |

#### Framework 3: CARE

Use when feedback is vague.

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **C** - Clarify | Ask for example |
| **A** - Ask | For specifics |
| **R** - Restate | What you heard |
| **E** - Extract | Action item |

**Example:** "You need to be more proactive."
**Response:** "Could you share an example of where you felt I could have taken more initiative?"

### When Feedback Feels Unfair

1. Regulate first
2. Ask for specific examples
3. Clarify expectations

> **Example:** "I may be misunderstanding. Could you walk me through a specific example so I can reflect properly?"

Professionalism includes knowing how to challenge respectfully.

### Asking for Feedback

**Why don't we ask?**
- We fear criticism
- We don't want to look incompetent
- We assume "no news is good news"
- UK culture often avoids unsolicited critique

#### How to Ask for Feedback

| Component | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **Context** | Anchor it in something specific: "Following yesterday's client presentation…" |
| **Focus** | Be clear: "I'm working on improving how I articulate business impact." |
| **Direction** | Ask forward-looking: "What's one thing I could strengthen next time?" |

#### Handling Vague Responses

If they say "You're doing fine":
- "I'm glad to hear that. If I wanted to elevate my impact further, what would you suggest?"
- "What would differentiate good from excellent in this area?"

### Cultural Differences in Feedback

In many UK corporate environments:
- Feedback is softened
- Politeness is prioritised
- Criticism may be implied rather than stated

| Indirect (Common in UK) | Direct (Common Elsewhere) |
|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| "You may want to revisit that." | "That presentation was unclear." |
| "That's interesting." | "Lead with the outcome next time." |
| "It might help to simplify." | "Simplify this." |

> Direct does not automatically mean disrespectful.

### Neurodiversity & Feedback

Not everyone processes feedback the same way. Neurodiversity can influence:
- How feedback is delivered
- How feedback is interpreted
- How emotional reactions show up
- How clarity is understood

**If you are neurodivergent receiving feedback:**
- Ask for explicit, concrete examples
- Request written follow-up
- Take time to process before responding if needed

**If giving feedback to someone neurodivergent:**
- Be explicit, not implied
- Avoid sarcasm or vague hints
- Focus on behaviour, not personality
- Provide clear next steps and confirm understanding

> Neurodiversity is not an excuse for unprofessional behaviour, but it is a reason to increase clarity, reduce assumption, and improve mutual understanding.

### Key Takeaways on Feedback

- Feedback is not a judgment of your character — it is data about your impact
- Your first reaction may be emotional; your second response is a choice
- Regulation is a skill; growth is a choice
- Clear expectations reduce anxiety
- Explicit examples improve performance
- Written follow-ups improve retention
- Behaviour-based language reduces misinterpretation

## Appendix: Communication Rubric

### Grading Criteria for "Tell Me About Yourself"

| Value | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **0** | Student didn't display this criteria at all; completely omitted |
| **1** | Clear effort but fell short in some ways; met unconvincingly or only in parts |
| **2** | Clearly met criteria; example of how it ought to be convincingly fulfilled |

### Criteria Areas

| Criterion | Level 0 | Level 1 | Level 2 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Mix of personal & professional stories** | Only one aspect | Briefly mentioned both but didn't link | Blended well; related passions back to professional interests |
| **Concise** | No takeaways or emphasis | Diverged to unrelated tangents | Revolved around key elements, returned to them |
| **Threaded** | All over place, jumping back and forth | Started following thread but diverged | Followed sensible, understandable thread |
| **Intro & Outro** | Missing or very weak | One missing or unclear | Grabbed attention with intro; strong, clear ending |
| **Pacing & Clarity** | Extremely rushed, unclear | Inconsistent pace | Fully clear, adequate pace, coherent ideas |
| **Non-verbal Communication** | Camera off, obscene background, reading script | Poor positioning, frozen, shuffling | Good centered posture, effective gesticulation |
| **Vocabulary** | Limited vocabulary, slang | Unprofessional terms or repetition | Adequate for setting, rich, good first impression |
| **Fillers** | Riddled with fillers, incomprehensible | Notable filler words | Very few to no filler words |
| **Natural** | Forced, overbearing or extremely timid | Some monotone or misplaced elements | Felt natural |
| **Engagement** | Too short or too long, unenjoyable | OK but dull/uninspired at times | Engaging, interesting, energizing |
